仮名{かな}（ＫＡＮＡ）

Kana refers to the symbols/character that make up the japanese language, these mostly refer to Hiragana and Katakana.

Firstly however, a good concept to learn before Hiragana and Katakana is about Rōmaji. Rōmaji is about using the Latin alphabet (English characters) to spell/sound out Japanese characters, for example the Japanese symbol ま{ma} is written out in Rōmaji as "ma" as that is a representation of what the character sounds like using Latin characters. When looking over the Rōmaji, you may begin to notice Japanese's unique lingustic structure of Consonant then Vowel (e.g. ka, sa, ta, na, etc), you may also notice that in the word "Rōmaji" the o has an accent on it (ō), this is to show that there is a long vowel sound, in Japanese this is called 長音{ちょうおん}. Words with long vowels are different to those without as 高校{こうこう} means high school whereas ここ{koko} means here. In Hiragana, the vowel sound can be extended by adding either an あ{a}, い{i}, or う{u}, depending on the vowel and it's constanent such as 高校{こうこう}. In Katakana these long vowels are indicated by "ー", for example in the word ラーメン{rāmen}

Hiragana are a simple cursive version of kanji characters with 46 basic characters, it was developed during the Heian Era (794-1185 AD) and initially known as 女手{おんなで} meaning "women's hand" as it was primarily used by women during this time who were excluded from learning kanji, over time Hiragana started to integrate into the japanese language and today now plays a vital part in the language, such as being used in furigana (a helping aid for spelling out kanji, also a feature of this app), gramatical elements such as particles and verb endings, and with words that are native to Japan such as こんにちは{konnichiwa}.

Katakana is a character set made up of straight, angular lines each of which 46 basic characters (the same as hiragana), Katakana was developed during the Heian Era (794-1185 AD) as a phonetic guide for Buddhist monks and scholars, and was develop from the radicals (indervidual symbols found within a kanji character) within 万葉仮名{まんようがな} (ancient Chinese characters used in Japan), which is where Katakana gets it's name as it translated to "fragmentary kana" as it is created from fragments of kanji characters, in modern Japanese Katakana represents Foreign Loanwords (also known as 外来語{がいらいご}), such as ビーチ{bīchi} which means beach and ホテル{hoteru} which means hotel, it's good to note taht even though these sound like English, not all loanwords do, Some can be shortened like in コンビニ{konbini} which means convenience store and some may not even be english such as パン{pan} which comes from the Portuguese word "pão" for bread, Katakana is also used in Onomatopoeia such as「ニャー{nyā}」(Cat's meow) or 「ドキドキ{dokidoki}」 which is the sound of a heartbeat, as well as many other uses like for technical or scientific terms.

Beyond the basic 46 characters, Hiragana and Katakana can have diagraphs (a pair of characters which creates a single distinct sound) and diacritics (a glyph/shape added to a character to change it's sound).

the diagraphs used in modern Japanese are called 拗音{ようおん}, and in modern japanese they normally take a kana ending with i (e.g. き{ki}, し{shi}, ち{chi}, に{ni}, etc) and add either や{ya}, ゆ{yu}, or よ{yo} to combind the 2 sounds together, for example, the word 今日{kyō} which translates to today (spelt out like きょう{kyō}) uses this, by putting the き{ki} and よ{yo} kanas together to create きょ{kyo}, note that や{ya}, ゆ{yu}, and よ{yo} when used in 拗音{ようおん} are made slightly smaller, like ゃ{ya}, ゅ{yu}, and ょ{yo}, when typing in Japanese using Rōmaji, you can make these small symbols by putting an "x" or an "l" before the Rōmaji, for example typing "xyo" will make "ょ{yo}"

There are rare circumstances where diagraphs occur with a different pair of kana which doesn't contain や{ya}, ゆ{yu}, or, よ{yo}, usaually in Katakana with loanwords, for example, カフェ{kafe} (meaning cafe) conatins the diagraph フェ{fe}, to make an easy rule for this, a diagraph is just the 2 kana sounds mixed together so フ{fu} and エ{e} makes フェ{fe}, there are also diagraphs such as and kana such as which aren't used in modern Japanese, if you are intrested to learn more you can search up about the history of Japanese diagraphs
