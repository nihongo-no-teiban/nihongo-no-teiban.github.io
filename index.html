仮名{かな}（ＫＡＮＡ）

仮名とは何ですか？{かなとはなんですか？}（ＷＨＡＴ　ＡＲＥ　ＫＡＮＡ？）

仮名{かな} refers to the phonetic symbols that make up indervidual sounds found in Japanese，of which there a 2 types of 仮名{かな} called 平仮名{ひらがな} and 片仮名{かたかな}．both types of 仮名{かな} have 46 basic symbols which are usaually presented in a table called 五十音{ごじゅうおん} which means "50 sounds" in Japanese (note that historically there were a total of 50 however some are not used in modern Japanese)．This table is then split into 5 collums (段{だん}) and 10 rows (行{ぎょう})，the collums represent the 5 vowel: a，i，u，e，and o and the 10 rows represent the constanent (apart from the first row which is just the vowels)， that are in Japanese: k，s，t，n，h，m，y，r，and w

ローマ字{ローマじ}（ＲＯＭＡＪＩ）

A good concept to learn before 平仮名{ひらがな} and 片仮名{かたかな} is about ローマ字{ローマじ}． Rōmaji (as it is spelt out using ローマ字{ローマじ}) is about using the Latin alphabet (English characters) to spell/sound out Japanese 仮名{かな} ，for example the Japanese 仮名{かな}，ま{ma}， is written out in ローマ字{ローマじ} as "ma" as that is a representation of what the character sounds like using Latin characters (in English)． ローマ字{ローマじ} is a great tool to help understand pronounciation as it allows English speakers to be able to pronounce Japanese 仮名{かな} easily，it is also good to note that in ローマ字{ローマじ} long vowels are indicated by a line above the vowel called a "macron"，e.g． ā，ī，ū，ē，ō (to find out more about long vowels you can look at the long vowels section later in the page)

Although ローマ字{ローマじ} is a great tool for learning the basics, most of this webpage will not use it (a part from the basics like 平仮名{ひらがな}， 片仮名{かたかな} and grammer elements) in order to help you recognise concepts like 漢字{かんじ} and grammer patterns found in Japanese

撥音{はつおん}（ＨＡＴＳＵＯＮ）

When look at a our 五十音{ごじゅうおん} table, you may see an extra row added called 撥音{はつおん}, although it isn't part of the original 五十音{ごじゅうおん} it is usually presented with them as it a part of basic the 仮名{がな} you will need to learn, the 撥音{はつおん} is a short and quick nasal sound, which historically came from an old type of kana (which was removed during the 1900s) called 変体仮名{へんたいがな} and was the 仮名{がな} for "mu" which was the shortened to the "n" sound, the 撥音{はつおん} is usaually an "n" sound (like it is displayed in the tables and in ローマ字{ローマじ}) however there are a few situations where it can sound different, firstly it will make a normal "n" sound if it comes before alveolar consonants (t, d, s, z, n) 撥音{はつおん} makes a "m" sound if it comes before bilabial consonants (b, p, m) such as 新聞{しんぶん} which means newspaper, an "ng" sound if it comes before velar consonants (k, g) such as 元気{げんき} which means healthy or energetic, if it is before a vowel or や{ya}, ゆ{yu}, or よ{yo}, the vowel becomes slightly nasalised as well such as in 簡易{かんい} which means simple or easy.

Note that although it is nice to know the lingistics of the 撥音{はつおん}, you will probably pick up the correct pronounciations as you learn the words it is found in, so don't worry over trying to memorise all of the situations

平仮名{ひらがな}（ＨＩＲＡＧＡＮＡ）

平仮名{ひらがな} is a character set made up of a simple cursive version of ancient kanji characters with a total of 46 basic characters， it was developed during the Heian Era (794-1185 AD) and initially known as 女手{おんなで} meaning "women's hand" as it was primarily used by women during this time who were excluded from learning kanji， over time Hiragana started to integrate into the japanese language and today now plays a vital part in the language，such as being used in furigana (a helping aid for spelling out kanji，also a feature of this app)，gramatical elements such as particles and verb endings， and with words that are native to Japan such as こんにちは{konnichiwa}．

Katakana is a character set made up of straight，angular lines each of which total 46 basic characters (the same as hiragana)，Katakana was developed during the Heian Era (794-1185 AD) as a phonetic guide for Buddhist monks and scholars，and was develop from the radicals (indervidual symbols found within a kanji character) within 万葉仮名{まんようがな} (ancient Chinese characters used in Japan)，which is where Katakana gets it's name as it translated to "fragmentary kana" as it is created from fragments of kanji characters．

In modern Japanese，Katakana represents Foreign Loanwords (also known as 外来語{がいらいご})，such as ビーチ{bīchi} which means beach and ホテル{hoteru} which means hotel． Its good to note that even though these sound similar to English，not all loanwords do，some can be shortened like in コンビニ{konbini} which means convenience store and some may not even be english such as パン{pan} which comes from the Portuguese word "pão" for bread，Katakana is also used in Onomatopoeia such as「ニャー{nyā}」which is a cat's meow or 「ドキドキ{dokidoki}」 which is the sound of a heartbeat，as well as many other uses like for technical or scientific terms．

Beyond the basic 46 characters，Hiragana and Katakana can have diagraphs (a pair of characters which creates a single distinct sound) and diacritics (a glyph/shape added to a character to change it's sound)．

the diagraphs used in modern Japanese are called 拗音{ようおん}， and in modern japanese they normally take a 仮名{かな} ending with i (e.g． き{ki}， し{shi}， ち{chi}， に{ni}， etc) and add either や{ya}， ゆ{yu}， or よ{yo} to combind the 2 sounds together，for example，the word 今日{kyō} which translates to today (spelt out like きょう{kyō}) uses this，by putting the き{ki} and よ{yo} 仮名{かな} together to create きょ{kyo}，note that や{ya}， ゆ{yu}， and よ{yo} when used in 拗音{ようおん} are made slightly smaller，like ゃ{ya}， ゅ{yu}， and ょ{yo}，(in Japanese these are known as 小さい仮名{ちいさいかな})when typing in Japanese using Rōmaji，you can make these small symbols by putting an "x" before the Rōmaji，for example typing "xyo" will make "ょ{yo}"

There are rare circumstances where diagraphs occur with a different pair of 仮名{かな} which doesn't contain や{ya}， ゆ{yu}， or， よ{yo}，usaually in Katakana with loanwords， for example，カフェ{kafe} (meaning cafe) conatins the diagraph フェ{fe}，to make an easy rule for this，a diagraph is just the 2 仮名{かな} sounds mixed together so フ{fu} and エ{e} makes フェ{fe}，there are also diagraphs such as くゎ{kwa} or くゐ{kwi} in hiragana and kana in general like ゐ{wi}/ ヰ{wi} and ゑ{we}/ ヱ{we} which aren't used in modern Japanese， if you are intrested to learn more you can search up about the history of Japanese

In Japanese，there are 2 main diacritics known as 濁点{だくてん} and 半濁点{はんだくてん}， 濁点{だくてん} is know as a voicing mark and is the most used diacritic in Japanese，symbolised by 2 dashes on the top-right of a kana，memorising diacritics in japanese is quite easy as they use the same 仮名{かな} but change the sound of the constant for example，when using 濁点{だくてん}， か{ka} becomes が{ga}， さ{sa} becomes ざ{za}， た{ta} becomes だ{sa}，and は{ha} becomes ば{ba}． this is the same with all of the vowels and their 仮名{かな} as well，meaning you don't have to memorise new kana，this is also in both Katakana and Hiragana． The next diacritic is 濁点{だくてん}， 濁点{だくてん} is a semi-voicing diacritic symbolised by a circle shape in the top-right of the kana，濁点{だくてん} also only appears in 1 set of kana，which is turning h_ to p_，e.g． は{ha} into ぱ{pa}，this is also the same in both katakana and with all the different vowels as well，similarly to the diagraphs there are also 仮名{かな} with diacritics that aren't used in modern Japanese such as か゚(nga)，ら゚(la)，わ゙(va)，you can search up about the history of Japanese to find out more about them

Now with the 46 basic characters，also know as (which means 50 sounds however not all 50 are used in modern Japanese)， plus the diagraphics and the diacritics all in modern Japanese Hiragana and Katakana each have a total of 104 sounds

長音{ちょうおん}（ＬＯＮＧ　ＶＯＷＥＬＳ）

In Japanese，long vowels are know as 長音{ちょうおん}． In Hiragana，the vowel sound can be extended by adding a vowel (あ、い、う、え、お) to the end of a sound， for example on the vowel and it's constanent such as 高校{こうこう} which means "high school"． In Katakana these long vowels are indicated by "ー"，for example in the word ラーメン{rāmen}， it is also good to note that 仮名{かな} can be used in both Hiragana and Katakana to express emotion or emphasis，for example えぇ？ (or えー？) which means Eeh? And when reading words in Rōmaji，a long vowel sound is represented by a dash above the vowel (know as a )
